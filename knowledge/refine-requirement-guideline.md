# Practical Guidelines for Refining Requirements

Here's a straightforward approach to refining requirements effectively:

## Start with the core questions

Before diving into details, clarify the fundamental aspects: What problem are you actually solving? Who will use this? What does success look like? These questions help you avoid building the wrong thing beautifully.

## Use the "5 Whys" technique

When a stakeholder presents a requirement, ask "why" repeatedly (usually about five times) to uncover the real need behind the stated request. Often what people ask for isn't what they actually need.

## Break down vague statements

Transform fuzzy requirements into specific, testable ones. Instead of "the system should be fast," specify "search results must return within 2 seconds for 95% of queries." Use concrete numbers, timeframes, and measurable criteria.

## Identify and document assumptions

Write down what you're assuming to be true. This prevents misunderstandings later when someone reveals an assumption you didn't know you were making.

## Create concrete examples and scenarios

Work through realistic user scenarios step-by-step. "When a customer attempts to purchase an out-of-stock item, the system should..." This reveals edge cases and clarifies expected behavior.

## Prioritize ruthlessly

Use a framework like MoSCoW (Must have, Should have, Could have, Won't have) to categorize requirements. This forces conversations about what's truly essential versus nice-to-have.

## Validate with prototypes or mockups

Show, don't just tell. Simple sketches or wireframes can reveal misunderstandings faster than pages of documentation.

## Review for conflicts and gaps

Cross-check requirements against each other to find contradictions. Look for missing pieces like security, performance, or error handling if they're not explicitly mentioned.
